The day you take delivery is the best day your paint will ever have

A new Tesla rolls out of the factory with paint in its purest possible state: no rock chips, no swirl marks, no embedded contamination, no water spots. Paint protection film does not repair damage; it locks in whatever condition the paint is in on the day we install it. That is the whole logic of timing: the sooner the film goes on, the closer to perfect the surface underneath stays, for as long as you own the car.

Wait a few weeks and the math changes. Even careful daily driving adds light swirls from washing, fine pitting from highway sand, and sooner or later the first chip. The film will still protect everything from that day forward, but it cannot undo what already happened. And anything that needs fixing first (paint correction before installation) adds time and cost that day-one owners simply skip.

No, you do not have to wait for the paint to cure

This is the most common myth we hear, and it comes from a real fact applied to the wrong situation. Resprayed panels from a body shop do need 30 to 90 days to outgas before film can go on. Factory paint is different: it is baked and fully cured in industrial ovens before the car ever leaves the production line. By the time you pick up your Tesla, the paint is ready. There is no waiting period. PPF can be installed the same week, even the same day you take delivery.

Why this matters more on a Tesla

Tesla's factory paint has a reputation for being on the softer side, which is part of why Model 3, Model Y, Model S and Model X owners see rock chips and swirl marks earlier than they expect. Soft paint is not a defect; it simply means the protection question is more urgent, not less. One note for Cybertruck owners: your panels are stainless steel, not paint, but film still earns its keep there, guarding the finish against scratches and scuffs.

And then there is Miami. Construction sand on the Palmetto, gravel kicked up on I-95, love bug season, and a sun that works on unprotected clear coat all year long. The first long drive on South Florida highways is exactly when a brand new front end meets its first debris field. That is why our honest answer to "how soon" is: before that drive, not after it.
